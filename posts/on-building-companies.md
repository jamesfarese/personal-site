# on building companies (and why i think they're art)

i've been building companies for long enough now that people ask me why. why keep doing it? why not just get a job somewhere comfortable and call it a day?

the honest answer: i think companies are art.

not metaphorically. not as a cute reframe. i mean it literally. a company is a creative act. it begins with someone looking at the world and thinking *it doesn't have to be like this.* that's the same impulse that makes a painter reach for a brush. the same thing that makes a writer sit down and start a sentence.

---

## what a company actually is

a company is a machine for getting people to cooperate toward a shared hallucination.

the hallucination is the product, the mission, the thing you're building. the machine is the culture, the org design, the incentives, the hiring. and the art is in making the machine and the hallucination reinforce each other.

bad companies have good hallucinations and bad machines. or bad hallucinations and good machines. the rare great companies have both.

---

## the medium matters

every medium has constraints. paint has texture and opacity. music has time and frequency. companies have people, capital, and time.

working within constraints is what makes art interesting. it's easy to imagine a perfect product with infinite money and infinite time. the craft is in building something true with what you actually have.

i find that constraint generative. the fact that you have to ship, have to hire, have to make payroll — it forces clarity. you learn what you actually believe.

---

## what i've learned

a few things, in no particular order:

**taste is a moat.** most companies are built on skills. the best ones are built on taste. taste is slower to develop and impossible to copy.

**culture is the product.** the internal experience you create for your team becomes the external experience your users have. it always leaks.

**momentum is real.** there's a physics to companies. objects in motion stay in motion. the hardest part is usually the first part — getting from zero to moving. once you're moving, a lot of problems solve themselves.

**slow down to speed up.** i've learned this one the hard way, multiple times. taking a week to think clearly is almost always worth it. it's the cowboy coding that kills you.
